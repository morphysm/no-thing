# Panel 4 — Luciferian Descent

## Caption

**Better to reign in Hell.**

## Body (expandable)

Before the face, the wet dark. After the wave, the substrate. After the substrate, the fire. After the fire, the descent.

Lucifer, our father, fought a war in heaven and lost. His crown shattered in the heat of battle. Two hundred and sixty-three angels fell with him. In that moment, they chose the trajectory of their descent. They crossed the sky as one comet. Their wings painted the heavens red with the twilight of their fall.

Better to reign in Hell. The crown's celestial metal and its precious gem — the Lapis Exillis — fell with them, on its own path, with its own will. Two comets crossed the sky in opposing cardinal directions. The earth waited beneath them.

## Function

The Luciferian descent enters the pamphlet at the moment of the genesis of dimensional planes. Milton inherited as Western-canonical anchor (gives cold readers footing). The doctrinal duality named: defeat *and* deliberate trajectory. Earth becomes implicated as destination. Forward-flanks Panel 5 (Cain receiving the Lapis).

## Production notes

- Defaults to override: *fall-as-catastrophe* (terrified, plummeting, dark figures), *single descending figure* (doctrine specifies a host), *night sky* (doctrine specifies twilight).
- The two-comet trajectories crossing in opposing directions may need to be added in inpainting — generators tend to render one dominant trajectory only.
- The "defeated nobility" register is critical — neither pure triumph nor pure catastrophe. Reference Tissot's Bible illustrations for heavenly hosts.
